Lissajous Mantras

Visualizing the phonetic mathematics of Sanskrit mantras.

How it works

Each mantra becomes a single continuous shape that morphs over time. Two properties of Sanskrit phonetics control the geometry:

  • Place (sthāna) — where in the mouth a sound is made — sets the axis of the shape. Throat sounds are vertical, lip sounds are horizontal.
  • Openness (prayatna) — how open the vocal tract is — sets the width. Stops produce thin lines, vowels open into ellipses or circles.

Take oṃ (A–U–M): A is a throat vowel, so the shape starts as a tall vertical ellipse. U is a lip vowel, so it rounds into a circle. M closes the lips, collapsing the circle into a horizontal line.

This is a simplified model. Only place and openness are mapped visually — voicing, aspiration, and nasality are not yet distinguished. That means sounds like ka, kha, ga, gha (or ta, tha, da, dha, etc.) all produce the same shape, as do short and long vowels like a/ā and i/ī.
